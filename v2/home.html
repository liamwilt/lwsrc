    Research Experience

    Immersive Virtual Environment Testing Area | Bethesda, MD

    01/2015 - Present | Lab Manager

    As laboratory manager of the Immersive Virtual Environment Testing Area on the main campus of the National Institutes of Health, I collaborate with intra- and extramural investigators seeking the use of our technologies in the design and development of research protocols. I am also responsible for the implementation of research experiments, management and analysis of data, as well as the integration and maintenance of lab technologies, including virtual or augmented reality software/hardware, EDA (skin conductance), EEG, or EKG, as well as facial expression-based emotion recognition software/hardware.

    American University Computer Science Department | Washington, DC

    5/2012 - 12/2014 | Research Technician

    With Dr. Bei Xiao, I developed a three-dimensional virtual world to investigate several psychophysical questions concerning the human understanding of fabric qualities (e.g. bend, spring, texture). The virtual world prompted users to compare and judge sets of fabrics on their visual properties and motion mechanics over 500 trials. For each trial and fabric, internal physics and texture components were modified. As a result, the motion mechanics and perceptual quality of each fabric was altered (i.e. some fabrics appeared as silk but behaved like denim, and vice versa, while under the influence of external forces). Participants were asked to judge which fabric of any given pair exhibited greater values of the bend or spring qualities. Data was examined using a multi-dimensional scale analysis.

    American University Vision Science Lab | Washington, DC

    12/2010 - 05/2012 | Research Assistant

    With Dr. Arthur Shapiro, I developed an interdisciplinary project studying the human perception of motion via virtual reality technologies. Drawing from extant evidence on retinal sensitivity to luminance flux and standard computational models for human motion perception, we created stimuli to investigate and advance an understanding of the conditions in which the percept of motion is possible. Utilizing a mathematical premise, we created stimuli to elicit three distinct types of motion perception.

    National Institute of Health | Bethesda, MD

    08/2007 - 08/2009 | Postbaccalaureate IRTA Fellow

    With Dr. Jiska Cohen-Mansfield, I investigated non-pharmacological treatment options for persons affected with Alzheimer's type dementia. Our goal was to create a software platform that catered to the social-behavioral needs of the affected individual by reducing physical or verbal agitation over the course of daily life. Via behavioral observation and interviews (e.g. with family, caretakers, and, if possible, with the patient), we created intervention strategies for each patient and delivered them with electronic tablets. Strategies included an array of stimuli, ranging from simulated social interactions, videos of personal value, exercises, or puzzles. Efficacy was measured after 4 weeks of implementation per participant.

    University of Maryland Motor Development Research Group | College Park, MD

    8/2005 - 05/2007 | Research Assistant

    With Dr. Jane Clark, I studied the visuomotor capabilities of children with and without developmental coordination disorder (DCD), evaluating their ability to integrate new visual information into a goal-oriented drawing task. Data were collected with a customized pressure-sensitive tablet and it was my charge to implement the tool, working with each child per trial, extracting data for analysis, and translating it for other research assistants.

    University of Maryland Child Development Lab | College Park, MD

    8/2005 - 05/2007 | Research Assistant

    With Dr. Nathan Fox, I investigated the development of spatiomotor reasoning in children between the ages of four and six. We evaluated three sets of data per participant: the reported plans children made to navigate a room of complex design, the physical path actually chosen to navigate the room, and their post-hoc conception of the path chosen (what they recalled of their path and choices). Each child arranged their post-hoc conceptions using a three-dimensional computer program that I co-designed and from which I drew the data for analysis.
